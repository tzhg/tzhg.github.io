More information
This visualisation compares income inequality between regions of various countries (more countries may be added later). I constructed the regions by combining administrative subdivisions (states) with similar culture, ensuring that the regions have similar populations. I gathered income distribution data for the states of each country, specifically for household pre-tax income in 2019 or 2020. I followed the method in Chotikapanich et. al to fit beta-2 distributions from the limited income distribution data available, and subsequently estimate regional income distributions.
Common measures of income inequality include the Gini coefficient, and ratios involving quantiles such as P90/P10 or S90/S10. I chose to visualise quantiles, as they are not affected by the extreme wealth which my fitted model may not estimate well. I chose the quantiles 1/6, 1/2, and 5/6, which can be interpreted as the median of the 0–1/3, 1/3–2/3, and 2/3–1 quantile intervals respectively.
There are a number of considerations to take into account before making any conclusions from this visualisation:
  • My figures are only estimates based on my model, and I make no guarantee on their accuracy
  • It is difficult to compare income inequality between countries, as countries may consider different sources of income
  • Income inequality may not take into account wealth, taxes and transfers, pension contributions or cost of living
Regardless of these limitations, I hope that this project can serve as an educational and thought-provoking introduction to this serious issue.

Data sources:
  • US household income in 2019: United States Census Bureau,
  • Mexico total household current income in 2020: Instituto Nacional de Estadística y Geografía,
  • Brazil total household income and change in household wealth in 2017–2018: Instituto Brasileiro de Geografia e Estatística.

How I spend my time
Proportion of time spent on various activities, smoothed
More information
Time is a precious resource. And what are we, but the sum of our actions? Some events in our life will stay in our minds forever. But most hours that we spend will be left behind, ceasing to exist as soon as the next hour begins.
In order to preserve a complete picture of my lifetime, I have been collecting data on each hour I spend since the beginning of 2017. I have categorised my activity into seven types by their purpose, and for each hour I record the category which best represents the activity during that hour. This visualisation shows the proportion of time each day spent on each category of activity (on the y-axis), over time (on the x-axis). These proportions are smoothed using a Gaussian filter.
The analysis is done using a Python script. It requires two input files: one describing the categories, and one file containing the data. To facilitate the recording of data throughout my day, I used AppSheet to create a mobile app through which I can easily add data to a Google Sheets spreadsheet. Once I convert this to a CSV file, I can append this data to my main data file using another Python script.
Despite the inadequacy of the necessary discretisation, both of time and type, against the intricate complexity of human activity, I believe that the generous degree of smoothing can allow some meaningful patterns and trends to emerge over the long-term. I take great joy and comfort in rediscovering the events and stages which defined my past, and establishing a framework with which I can set goals for the future.

Made using:
  • jQuery,
  • NumPy,
  • pandas.

More information
Election results can be visualised using cartograms, which convey the proportions of seats won by each party, through a map projection which associates area to the number of seats, while retaining some geographical information on the constituencies. See for example this map of the 2017 UK general election.
Ireland's general elections (GEs) use multi-member constituencies to elect TDs, members of parliament, to Dáil Éireann, its national assembly. Cartograms for this type of election can be trickier to make, as there may be additional constraints due to seats being grouped by constituency. I constructed my map projections by hand, aiming to preserve the shapes and locations of constituencies as much as possible, and additionally grouping the constituencies by subregion (province) to further facilitate spatial orientation.
TDs are elected by a single transferable voting system, where voters rank the candidates in their constituency by order of preference. Tooltips on the visualisation show the percentage of first preference votes won by each TD. One TD, the Ceann Comhairle, or chairperson, is automatically re-elected, and is marked "C.C.".

More information
Climate data recorded at various locations can be effectively visualised by colouring in a map of the region with colours associated to values (for example this map of annual mean temperature). However, this requires a dense collection of data points in order to estimate the variable over the entire surface. Using such a geographic mapping on a smaller data set creates unavoidable gaps between the data points, wasting space and hindering comparison.
This problem can be solved by creating a new mapping, where the data points are positioned according to similarity of the data. To test this idea, I collected climate data from Wikipedia on a selection of cities (corresponding to the 141 alpha and beta level global cities as of 2020). To create the similarity mapping, I used a self-organising map, an algorithm which produces a discrete topology-preserving lower-dimensional representation of an input space. I chose 4 climate variables, and I considered their values for each month as independent, resulting in 48 dimensions of data. As the algorithm can create maps of any rectangular shape (with enough area), I generated multiple maps to ensure that there is a suitable visualisation no matter the screen-size. I also ensured that the resulting mappings were injective in order to facilitate labelling. The resulting visualisation is compact, adaptable, and allows for better understanding of the data (including possibility for clustering) than a regular map.

Made using:
  • jQuery,
  • NumPy,
  • minisom (SOM implementation in Python),
  • cmcrameri (colour palettes).
