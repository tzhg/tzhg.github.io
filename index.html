How I've spent my time
Time spent on various activities, smoothed
More information
Time is a precious resource. And what are we, but the sum of our actions? Some events in our life will stay in our minds forever. But most hours that we spend will be left behind, ceasing to exist as soon as the next hour begins.
In order to preserve a complete picture of my lifetime, I have been collecting data on each hour I spend since the beginning of 2017. This visualisation shows the proportion of time each day spent on various types of activities, smoothed using a Gaussian filter.
This data gives me great insight into my past, as I can identify various events and stages of my life, and allows me to set goals for the future. Hopefully other people will find it interesting too.

Election results can be visualised using cartograms, which convey the proportions of seats won by each party, through a map projection which associates area to the number of seats, while retaining some geographical information on the constituencies. See for example this map of the 2017 UK general election.
Ireland's general elections use multi-member constituencies to elect TDs, members of parliament, to Dáil Éireann, its national assembly. Cartograms for this type of election can be trickier to make, as there may be additional constraints due to seats being grouped by constituency. I constructed my map projections by hand, aiming to preserve the shapes and locations of constituencies as much as possible, and additionally grouping the constituencies by subregion (province) to further facilitate spatial orientation.
TDs are elected by a single transferable voting system, where voters rank the candidates in their constituency by order of preference. Tooltips on the visualisation show the percentage of first preference votes won by each TD. One TD, the Ceann Comhairle, or chairperson, is automatically re-elected, and is marked "C.C.".

How city climates compare

More information
Climate data recorded at various locations can be effectively visualised by colouring in a map of the region with colours associated to values (for example this map of annual mean temperature). However, this requires a dense collection of data points in order to estimate the variable over the entire surface. Using such a geographic mapping on a smaller data set creates unavoidable gaps between the data points, wasting space and hindering comparison.
This problem can be solved by creating a new mapping, where the data points are positioned according to similarity of the data. To test this idea, I collected climate data from Wikipedia on a selection of cities (corresponding to the 141 alpha and beta level global cities as of 2020). To create the similarity mapping, I used a self-organising map, an algorithm which produces a discrete topology-preserving lower-dimensional representation of an input space. I chose 4 climate variables, and I considered their values for each month as independent, resulting in 48 dimensions of data. As the algorithm can create maps of any rectangular shape (with enough area), I generated multiple maps to ensure that there is a suitable visualisation no matter the screen-size. I also ensured that the resulting mappings were injective in order to facilitate labelling. The resulting visualisation is compact, adaptable, and allows for better understanding of the data (including possibility for clustering) than a regular map.
